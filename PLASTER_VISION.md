# PLASTER — Vision, Values & Product Soul
This document captures the why behind every decision. Read alongside PLASTER.md.

---

## What Plaster Is

Plaster is a living city wall. It is the telephone pole outside the venue, the cork board at the record store, the flyer stuffed in your jacket pocket that you forgot about until it fell out three days later and you thought — wait, that's tonight. It is Portland's cultural life made visible and beautiful and shareable.

It is not a ticketing platform. It is not a calendar app. It is not Eventbrite with better fonts. It is a place where the art of going out — the discovery, the anticipation, the shared excitement, the memory of having been there — lives as a first-class citizen.

---

## The Problem It Solves

Portland has one of the richest live music and arts scenes in the country. Holocene, Revolution Hall, Mississippi Studios, The 1905, Polaris Hall, Showbar — these are not just venues, they are institutions. The artists who play them, the regulars who show up for them, the communities that form around them — this is real cultural infrastructure.

But right now that culture is scattered. It lives in Instagram stories that disappear, in Facebook event pages nobody checks, in word-of-mouth chains that break. The poster on the telephone pole has more presence than any of it.

Plaster gives that culture a home. One beautiful, scrollable, living wall where Portland's event art is treated with the respect it deserves. Where you can feel the city's pulse just by looking at it.

## Core Values

### Anti-extractive design
Plaster does not harvest attention. It does not manufacture anxiety. It does not use dark patterns to keep you scrolling past the point of joy. Every design decision asks: does this serve the person using it, or does it serve the platform? The platform serves the person. Always.

Ads exist in Plaster — but only tasteful local ads, only between forum posts and on the RSVP completion screen. Never on the wall. Never on the exchange screen. Never in the chat. The wall is sacred. The experience of discovering a show is sacred.

### The poster as art
Event posters are one of the last great vernacular art forms. They are designed under pressure, printed in runs of a few hundred, stapled to poles in the rain, and gone in a week. Plaster treats them as the art objects they are. Full bleed. Sampled color backdrops that echo the poster's own palette. No UI chrome on the art itself at high zoom levels. The poster speaks for itself.

### Community over consumption
The goal is not to get you to buy more tickets. The goal is to connect you to your city and to the people in it. The LINE UP screen exists not because we want to track your behavior but because knowing what your friends are going to — and feeling the excitement of your own upcoming nights stacked up like a queue of diamonds — is genuinely joyful. That joy is the product.

### Local first, always
Plaster launches in Portland. It may grow. But it will never lose the texture of a specific place. The venues are real. The shows are real. The neighborhoods matter. The app should feel like it could only exist here, even if it eventually exists everywhere.

## Design Philosophy

### The wall is the thing
Everything else — the map, the LINE UP, the messaging — orbits the wall. The wall is the product's heartbeat. It should always feel alive. Full of color and personality and the specific energy of a city that takes its nights seriously.

### Night mode is the default
Plaster is a nightlife app. The default dark theme — almost black (#0c0b0b), warm off-white text (#f0ece3) — is not a trendy choice. It is the correct choice. You look at this app in a dark room, on your way to a show, standing outside a venue. The theme switches via a swipe on the wordmark, a gesture that feels like flipping a light switch.

### Analogue texture in a digital space
The design language reaches toward the physical world. The knurl wheel on the map scrubber feels machined. The diamond shapes feel cut, not rendered. The chip carousel feels like flipping through a physical card index. The date blocks feel like rubber stamps. Every interaction should have a sense of weight and materiality even though it is made of light.

### Typography as identity
Playfair Display 900 for the wordmark and headings — it has the drama of a poster headline. Barlow Condensed 700 for labels and chips — the compressed urgency of a show bill. Space Grotesk for body text — friendly, legible, modern without being cold. These three fonts together have a specific personality. They are not interchangeable.

### The sampled backdrop
When a poster is uploaded, Plaster samples the four corner pixels and generates a conic gradient backdrop. This means every poster card has its own unique atmospheric halo — the colors of the poster bleeding into the space around it. This is a core design feature. It must never be removed. It makes the wall feel alive in a way that a plain black or white background never could.

### Diamonds
The diamond shape is Plaster's signature motif. It appears in the LINE UP queue, in the feed avatars, in the profile pictures. It is a square standing on its tip — stable and dynamic at the same time. It feels like a marquee, like a suit of cards, like something you might see on the back of a leather jacket at a show. It is not precious but it is distinctive.

## The LINE UP Screen

The LINE UP is about anticipation. It is about the pleasure of having things to look forward to. When you "I'll be there" a show on the wall, that show's poster joins a queue of diamonds on the right side of your LINE UP screen — stacked up, waiting. As you scroll through the activity feed, each diamond slides into place when you reach its date. The queue getting longer is exciting. The diamonds dropping into place is satisfying.

The activity feed is the social layer — not algorithmic, not optimized, just the honest activity of people and places you follow. Your friend is going to that show. Holocene just added a date. glitterqueen was crowned Most Likely to Know Every Word. These are the conversations that used to happen on the telephone pole and at the record store. Plaster just gives them a beautiful place to live.

The LINE UP panel slides in from the right because it is yours — a personal drawer. Profile panels slide in from the left because you are stepping into someone else's world. This spatial logic is intentional and should be maintained throughout the app.

---

## The Social Layer

Plaster's social features are built around going out together, not performing for an audience.

**Groups** are your real-world social clusters — your book club, your trivia crew, your best friends. You can message them, share shows with them, see when they're going to the same thing you are.

**Profiles** are simple and honest. Who you are, where you've been, what superlatives you hold. Not a broadcast platform. A record of your nights.

**Superlatives** are playful and community-created. Queen of the Crystal Ballroom. Most Likely to Know the Opener. These emerge from attendance patterns and community recognition. They are badges of honor for the regulars.

**Messaging** is utilitarian and joyful. You should be able to send someone a show and say "we should go to this" and add people to the thread and make it happen. The app facilitates the thing, it doesn't become the thing.

---

## Venue and Artist Accounts

Venues and artists are not advertisers. They are community members. A venue account can post updates, share videos (via YouTube/Vimeo links — no hosting), announce shows. These posts appear in the LINE UP feed alongside friend activity — not in a separate sponsored section, not with special treatment. They earn their place in the feed the same way everyone else does: by being worth paying attention to.

---

## Monetization Philosophy

Plaster makes money through tasteful local advertising — placed between forum posts and on the RSVP completion screen. Not on the wall. Not in the chat. Not in the LINE UP feed. The experience of using the app is not for sale. The moment of discovery, the anticipation, the connection — these are protected.

Rob has described this as anti-extractive design. The app should give more than it takes. It should make you want to go out more, not spend more time on your phone.

---

## Beta Launch Strategy

Plaster launches in Portland with the book and music community first. These are communities that already know how to show up for things, already have taste and curiosity, and already have the social infrastructure to spread something they love. The wall needs to feel full and alive before it is shown to the world. Every poster that gets added is a vote of confidence in the idea.

---

## What Plaster Is Not

- Not a ticketing platform (though it links to tickets)
- Not a social media platform (though it has social features)
- Not an algorithm-driven feed (though it surfaces activity)
- Not a discovery engine built to maximize engagement
- Not Eventbrite
- Not Instagram for shows
- Not trying to be national before it is excellent locally

---

## The Feeling

You open Plaster and you feel the city. You see a poster for something you didn't know was happening and you think — wait, that's this Friday. You see that your friend is going. You tap "I'll be there" and a diamond joins your queue. You close the app and go get ready.

That is the whole product. That is what every decision should serve.
